Backbone & Circuits

How real networks scale: from your office router up through the ISP backbone, and back down to another business across the country.

01 · The Big Picture

ISP Backbone Hierarchy

A "backbone" is just the high-capacity core that carries traffic between networks. ISPs are stacked in tiers — your office connects to a small local ISP, that ISP rides on a regional carrier, and the regional rides on a global Tier 1. Watch a packet travel up and back down.

Read it: A packet from Site A enters the local ISP (PE = Provider Edge), climbs to a regional Tier 2, traverses the Tier 1 backbone (P routers do label-switching only — they don't know who you are), then descends to Site B. The customer-facing edge is the only place that "knows" the customer.

CCNA note: You don't need to memorize tier definitions for the exam — just the shape. Cisco wants you to know that the public Internet is hierarchical and your branch traffic flows up through providers, across a backbone, and down to the destination.
02 · Hardware on the Path

Devices Between You & the Backbone

Every WAN circuit hands off through a chain of devices. The exam asks you to recognize what each does and which OSI layer it lives at.

Demarcation point (often the modem or NID) is the legal boundary. Past it = ISP responsibility. Before it = your responsibility.

Switch

LAN forwarding

Forwards Ethernet frames inside a building based on MAC addresses. Trunks carry VLANs. Doesn't talk to the WAN — that's the router's job.

Layer 2

Router

WAN gateway

Decides where IP packets go next. The branch router that connects to the ISP is called the CE (Customer Edge) router.

Layer 3

Firewall

Security boundary

Filters traffic between zones. Modern next-gen firewalls (NGFW) inspect up through Layer 7. Often sits inline between LAN and WAN router.

Layer 3 / 4 / 7

Modem

Media converter

Translates between the ISP's medium (cable RF, DSL, fiber) and Ethernet. Cable modem · DSL modem · ONT (fiber) are all "modems" in CCNA terms.

Layer 1 / 2

NID

Demarcation

Network Interface Device. Telco-installed box that marks the demarc on metro-Ethernet or leased-line circuits. Smart-NIDs run loopback tests for the carrier.

Layer 1 / 2

CSU/DSU

Legacy serial

Channel Service Unit / Data Service Unit. Connects routers to T1/T3 leased lines. Mostly replaced by metro-E and fiber, but still on the CCNA blueprint as legacy reference.

Layer 1

PE Router

Provider Edge

The ISP's first router. Pushes MPLS labels, applies QoS, runs BGP with the customer (if needed). Customer never logs into this.

Layer 3

P Router

Provider core

Backbone label-switching router. Doesn't know individual customers — only swaps MPLS labels at line rate.

Layer 3 (label-switched)

Wireless AP

LAN edge

Bridges Wi-Fi clients onto the wired LAN. In a campus, APs talk to a wireless LAN controller (WLC) over a tunnel.

Layer 1 / 2
03 · Circuit Types

How Sites Reach the Backbone

A "circuit" is the physical or virtual link a business buys from a carrier. CCNA expects you to recognize each by name, speed range, and use case. Animated dot = packet on the wire.

Leased Line (T1 / T3 / E1)

1.5 Mbps – 45 Mbps · dedicated
Old-school dedicated copper. Symmetric, guaranteed bandwidth, expensive. Mostly replaced by Metro-E but still tested.

MPLS L3VPN

10 Mbps – 10 Gbps · private
Carrier-private network with QoS guarantees. Each site gets a virtual link to every other site. Premium WAN — banks, hospitals, multi-site enterprises.

Metro Ethernet

10 Mbps – 100 Gbps · LAN-feel WAN
Ethernet-over-fiber inside a metro area. Plug Ethernet into a NID — it's like a giant LAN. Fastest-growing WAN type.

Broadband Cable

100 Mbps – 1 Gbps · shared
Coax cable + DOCSIS modem. Asymmetric (download >> upload). Shared with the neighborhood — speed varies by load.

DSL

5 Mbps – 100 Mbps · phone line
Digital Subscriber Line — runs over phone copper. ADSL = asymmetric. Distance from the central office kills speed. Legacy in most markets.

Fiber to Premises (FTTP)

100 Mbps – 10 Gbps · dedicated optical
Fiber all the way to the building. Symmetric, low latency. Standard for new business installs. Modem here is the ONT.

Cellular (4G LTE / 5G)

50 Mbps – 1 Gbps · wireless WAN
Cellular modem with SIM card. Used for branch backup, IoT, mobile sites. 5G makes this competitive with wired broadband.

Internet VPN (IPsec)

any speed · cheap, encrypted
Encrypted tunnel between two routers across the public Internet. Site-to-site VPN, DMVPN. Replaces leased lines for budget-conscious branches.

05 · Inside the Router

Control Plane vs Data Plane

Every router (and every backbone, by extension) runs two parallel jobs. The control plane figures out where things are. The data plane moves packets at line rate. CCNA blueprint section 1.7 — high-yield.

Control Plane slow · rare

Routers talk to each other to build a map of the network. Routing protocols run here.

  • OSPF — interior, link-state, fast convergence
  • EIGRP — Cisco interior, distance-vector hybrid
  • BGP — between ISPs (Internet's routing protocol)
  • STP, ARP, ICMP — also control-plane traffic

Data Plane fast · constant

Routers forward packets based on the table the control plane built. Hardware-accelerated on real switches/routers.

  • FIB (Forwarding Information Base) — pre-computed lookup table
  • CEF (Cisco Express Forwarding) — Cisco's hardware-fast forwarding engine
  • ACL match · QoS marking · NAT translation — also data-plane work
  • If the control plane dies, data plane keeps forwarding (briefly) using its last-known table
Memory hook: Control plane = brain (thinks, decides, slow). Data plane = muscle (acts, forwards, fast). SDN (software-defined networking) is the idea of taking the brain off the router entirely and putting it on a central controller.
