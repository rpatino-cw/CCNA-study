03 · Cable Anatomy

Which Pairs Carry Power

RJ-45 has four twisted pairs. Data traditionally runs on two of them; the other two used to be "spare." PoE can deliver power over the data pairs (Mode A), the spare pairs (Mode B), or all four pairs (4PPoE).

Mode A — power on data pairs

  • Power rides on pins 1,2 and 3,6 as common-mode DC.
  • Used by most endspan PSEs — PoE built into the switch itself.
  • Data and power share the same pairs without interfering.

Mode B — power on spare pairs

  • Power rides on pins 4,5 and 7,8 — the "spare" pairs in 10/100 Ethernet.
  • Used by midspan PSEs — PoE injectors placed between a non-PoE switch and the PD.
  • Doesn't work in gigabit networks that use all 4 pairs for data — which is why 1 Gb networks shifted toward 4PPoE.

4-Pair PoE (802.3bt / UPoE)

  • All 8 conductors deliver power — current halved per wire.
  • Lower I²R losses, less heat in the bundle, required for 60W+ deliveries.
  • Cat 5e minimum; Cat 6A recommended for Class 7–8 at 100 m.
04 · Handshake

How the Switch Decides to Send Power

A PSE never sends full voltage first. It detects, classifies, then powers — protecting non-PoE devices from being fried.

1
Detection

PSE sends a small voltage (2.7–10 V) and measures the PD's signature resistance. Valid PDs show 25 kΩ ± 1%. Anything outside that range and the PSE refuses to power — a regular PC stays safe.

2
Classification

PSE bumps voltage to 15.5–20.5 V. PD replies with a current signature that maps to Class 0–8. The switch now knows how much power to reserve.

3
Power-up

Full voltage (44–57 V) applied. The PD starts drawing current within a bounded inrush profile.

4
Refinement via LLDP-MED / CDP

Once the link is up, the PD can renegotiate a more precise wattage using LLDP-MED TLVs or CDP (Cisco). This is how an AP tells the switch "I need exactly 23.4 W" instead of reserving the full class.

5
Monitoring

PSE watches current continuously. If the PD stops drawing — or yanks the cable — the switch removes power within ~400 ms and reclaims that budget for another port.

06 · Powered Devices

What Runs on PoE in the Real World

The CCNA exam loves asking which devices are PoE-capable. These are the common ones you should recognize on sight.

Voice

IP Phones

Cisco 7800/8800 series, desk phones, conference units. Typically Class 2–3 (7–15 W). Use LLDP-MED voice VLAN TLV to auto-configure.

Wireless

Access Points

Catalyst 9100, Aironet, Meraki APs. Wi-Fi 5 = Class 4 (30 W). Wi-Fi 6/6E = Class 5–6 (45–60 W). Wi-Fi 7 often needs 802.3bt Class 8.

Security

IP Cameras

Fixed cameras run on Class 2–3. PTZ (pan-tilt-zoom) cameras with heaters and IR illuminators need Class 4+ (PoE+).

IoT

Door Readers & Locks

Badge readers, electric strikes, intercoms. Low draw (Class 1–2) but critical — often paired with UPS-backed PoE switches.

IoT

Environmental Sensors

Temp, humidity, air-quality, occupancy sensors. Sub-4 W. Exam trick: IoT devices often run PoE even when labeled "wireless."

Display

Digital Signage & Thin Clients

Small LCDs, kiosks, zero-clients. Needs 802.3bt Type 3/4 or UPoE. Powers both the panel and the compute unit via one cable.

Building

LED Lighting & Shades

Low-voltage LED fixtures and motorized blinds — each 10–25 W. Tracked in the PoE budget just like any other PD.

Edge

Compact Compute

Raspberry Pi with PoE HAT, small industrial PCs, 5G femtocells. Increasingly common on factory floors and remote sites.

07 · Cisco Power Tech

Cisco-Specific Power Features

These are the names Cisco uses on their switches. On the exam, recognize what each does and which switch lines support it.

Delivery

UPoE / UPoE+

Cisco's 4-pair power on Catalyst 4500/9300/9400. UPoE = 60 W; UPoE+ = 90 W. Pre-dated 802.3bt.

Redundancy

StackPower

Catalyst 3750-X/9300 switches stack and share a combined PoE pool. One switch's spare capacity can feed another switch's PDs. Two modes: power-sharing (default) and redundant (one PSU held as backup).

Redundancy

RPS (Redundant Power System)

External RPS-2300 unit. If the switch's internal PSU fails, the RPS takes over — including PoE delivery. Protects access-layer switches in wiring closets.

Control

Static vs Dynamic Allocation

power inline static reserves class-based wattage regardless of actual draw. power inline auto (default) lets LLDP/CDP refine the reservation. Use static when oversubscription is a risk.

Policing

power inline police

Limits a port's draw. If a PD exceeds the cap, the switch either logs (log) or shuts the port (errdisable). Critical in budget-constrained deployments.

Monitoring

show power inline

The command you'll run in every lab. Shows per-port class, admin state, operational state, allocated W, and the switch's total budget. Know this output cold.

08 · Beyond the Switch

PSUs, PDUs & UPS — Facility Power

The exam brushes against facility power when it asks about resilience. Know the layers between the wall and the PD.

Layer 1

Dual PSUs

Enterprise switches ship with two hot-swap power supplies, usually fed by separate circuits. Loss of one circuit doesn't drop the switch. Both PSUs can combine to increase the PoE budget.

Layer 2

PDU (Power Distribution Unit)

Rack-mounted strip that feeds every device in the cabinet. Basic PDUs are dumb outlets; metered PDUs report draw; switched PDUs let you reboot individual outlets remotely.

Layer 3

UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply)

Battery-backed unit that bridges the gap until generators start. Critical for keeping switches — and therefore IP phones, security, and access control — online during utility outages.

Layer 4

Generators & ATS

Diesel or natural-gas generators kick in within ~10 s. An automatic transfer switch moves load from utility to generator. You'll see this in data-center questions, not access-switch questions.

A+B

Dual-Feed / A+B Power

Critical racks get two independent power paths — A-side and B-side — from separate UPS/PDU trees. Dual-PSU devices plug one cord into each. Any single failure is invisible.

Thermal

Power → Heat

Every watt pulled becomes heat. Dense PoE deployments can stress closet cooling — a 740 W switch with 600 W of active PoE draw puts ~2,500 BTU/hr into the room.

09 · Exam Tips

What to Memorize for Test Day

Fast-recall power facts

  • 802.3af → 15.4 W at PSE, 12.95 W at PD. Mnemonic: "af" = "af-fordable, fifteen."
  • 802.3at → 30 W at PSE, 25.5 W at PD. "PoE+" → one plus, times two → 30.
  • 802.3bt Type 3 → 60 W, Type 4 → 100 W. 4-pair only.
  • Mode A = power on data pairs (1,2 / 3,6). Mode B = power on spare pairs (4,5 / 7,8).
  • Endspan = PoE built into the switch. Midspan = external injector.
  • PSE = the switch. PD = the powered device. Never confuse them.
  • A PSE does detection → classification → power-up, then refines with LLDP-MED or CDP.
  • Cisco proprietary: UPoE (60 W), UPoE+ (90 W), Inline Power (7 W, legacy).
  • StackPower pools PoE across stacked switches. Two modes: power-sharing and redundant.
  • The budget is per-switch, not per-port. A 370 W switch can't light 24 ports at 30 W each.
  • PoE runs over copper Ethernet only — never over fiber.
