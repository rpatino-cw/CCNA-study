Origin

How the CCNA came to be

In 1993, Cisco introduced the CCIE — the Cisco Certified Internetwork Expert. It was, and still is, brutal. A two-day lab. Pass rates that hovered at single digits. The cert that minted network gods.[1]

Five years later, Cisco realized they had a problem. The internet was exploding. Every Fortune 500 was buying Cisco gear. But there was no way to tell whether the person racking the switch actually knew what they were doing. CCIE was a mountain. They needed a doorway.

So in 1998, at the Philadelphia Networker show, Cisco unveiled the CCNA — Cisco Certified Network Associate. Entry-level. A few months of study. One exam. The doorway worked.[1][2]

For ten years it stayed simple — one CCNA. Then in 2008, Cisco split it into specializations: CCNA Security, CCNA Voice, CCNA Wireless. The thinking was reasonable: networking was branching. The execution was a maze. Candidates had to pick a track before they understood what the tracks even meant.[1]

On February 24, 2020, Cisco reversed course. All specializations folded back into a single exam: 200-301. The reasoning was strategic — modern networking demanded hybrid skills (security, automation, wireless) and a unified entry credential reflected that. Today's CCNA is the consolidated one. Romeo's exam. The version on this site.[2][3]

The Number 256

Why IP addresses stop at 255

An IPv4 address is four numbers, each 0–255, separated by dots: 192.168.1.1. Why those numbers? Why four? Why stop at 255?

The answer goes back further than the internet itself.

The 8-bit byte: a 1964 decision

Before 1964, computers had no standard byte size. IBM machines used 6-bit bytes. Some used 7. Some used 9. The word "byte" was coined in 1956 by Werner Buchholz at IBM, designing the Stretch supercomputer.[4]

On April 7, 1964, IBM announced the System/360. It replaced all five of IBM's prior product lines with one strictly compatible family — and standardized the 8-bit byte across all of them. Within a decade, the entire computing industry had followed.[5][4]

By the time the internet's designers sat down to spec IPv4 in September 1981 (RFC 791), the 8-bit byte was the universal unit of computing. Memory was sold in 8-bit chunks. Networks were 8-bit wide. The choice to slice a 32-bit address into four 8-bit chunks wasn't a creative decision — it was the only sensible one.[6]

Why "octet" instead of "byte"?

Read RFC 791 and you won't see the word "byte." You'll see "octet."

Internet engineers in 1981 had just lived through the byte-size wars. They didn't trust the word. So they used a precise term: octet, from the Latin octo, meaning eight. An octet is exactly eight bits. No ambiguity, no exceptions.[7]

It's why you write 192.168.1.1 with four numbers. It's why each one stops at 255 (the largest number you can fit in 8 bits: 2^8 - 1). It's why CCNA candidates worldwide drill subnetting in groups of 8.

We don't write IP addresses in 4 chunks because someone thought it looked nice. We write them that way because IBM picked 8-bit bytes for the System/360 in 1964 and the entire computing industry inherited it. know
A short history of the internet

From "LO" to TLS

The internet has three plausible birthdays, depending on who you ask.

October 29, 1969
First ARPANET message
Charles Kline at UCLA tries to log into the SRI computer at Stanford. He types "L." It sends. He types "O." It sends. He types "G." SRI crashes. The first message ever sent over a packet-switched network was the two letters: LO.[8]
1972
Email
Ray Tomlinson at BBN introduces network email. Picks @ as the separator because it wasn't used in any name yet. The decision sticks for the next 53 years.[8]
September 1981
RFC 791 — IPv4 published
The IETF publishes the spec. 32-bit addresses. Four octets. Class A/B/C network divisions. The protocol that still routes 90% of the world's traffic in 2026.[6]
January 1, 1983
"The internet's birthday"
ARPANET cuts over from NCP to TCP/IP. This date is the most commonly cited birthday of the internet — the moment networks of networks could speak the same language.[8]
1990
HTML
Tim Berners-Lee at CERN invents HyperText Markup Language. He needs it because nobody can find anything on the network. <a href> changes everything.[8]
August 6, 1991
World Wide Web goes public
CERN posts a notice to alt.hypertext: "If you're interested in using the code, mail me." The web is open.[8]
1998
CCNA launches
Cisco introduces the Certified Network Associate at Networker. The exam this site exists to help you pass.[1]
February 2011
IPv4 exhaustion
IANA hands out the last block of IPv4 addresses. The 1981 designers thought 4.3 billion would last. They didn't predict mobile phones.

The TCP/IP stack that the CCNA tests on hasn't fundamentally changed since 1983. The protocols you'll learn — TCP, UDP, IP, ARP, DHCP, DNS — all predate or barely postdate the dotcom bubble. The vocabulary is the same. The packets look the same. That's a feature, not a bug.

The exam in 2026

What the CCNA actually is today

One exam. 200-301 v1.1. 120 minutes. ~100 questions. About $300 to take it. You either pass or you don't — Cisco's scaled scoring puts the cut between 800 and 850 out of 1000, depending on which version of the exam you happened to draw.[3]

The domains:

  • Network fundamentals — OSI, TCP/IP, cabling, IPv4/IPv6, subnetting
  • Network access — VLANs, trunking, EtherChannel, STP, wireless
  • IP connectivity — routing, OSPF, static routes, gateway redundancy
  • IP services — DHCP, DNS, NAT, NTP, SNMP, syslog, QoS
  • Security fundamentals — port security, ACLs, AAA, VPN concepts
  • Automation & programmability — REST, JSON, Ansible/Terraform/Python concepts

That last domain — automation — is what 2020 added. Cisco's bet was that the next decade of networking would not look like the last. They were right.

How long it takes

Speedruns & typical study times

A 1,300-IT-pro survey put the average at ~5 months of dedicated study to pass.[10] The honest range is wider:

  • Under 30 days — possible only with prior network experience and 4–6 hours/day. Bootcamp ($$$ + intense). Most "I passed in 2 weeks" Reddit posts hide years of pre-existing IT background.
  • 1–3 months — IT pros with adjacent skills (Net+, A+, 2+ years help-desk).
  • 3–6 months — the median for a working professional studying nights and weekends. Romeo's lane.
  • 6–12 months — career changers with no prior IT experience.

The money + the leverage

What a CCNA is actually worth

The headline number depends on who's surveyed:

  • Entry-level CCNA holder: $75,000–$92,000/yr (PayScale, Talent.com, multiple aggregators).[11][12]
  • Mid-level CCNA: ~$112,000/yr.[13]
  • Computer Network Architects (BLS, the senior path): $130,390/yr average. This is what a CCNA + 5 years of experience grows into.[14]

The interesting number is the premium over CompTIA Network+: roughly $15,000+/year.[13] That's the price of being vendor-specific in a world where most enterprise networks run Cisco gear. CCNA reads as "I can sit at a Cisco CLI tomorrow." Net+ reads as "I understand networks in theory."

The real value is leverage, not salary. CCNA opens doors:

  • Internal promotion paths in companies running Cisco infrastructure
  • Eligibility for security clearance + DOD networking roles
  • The required prerequisite for CCNP (the senior cert that pays $110–140K)
  • A signal that lets recruiters skip the "do they know what a VLAN is" round

It is, by ROI, one of the best certs in the entire IT ladder: ~$300 in fees + 3–6 months of study yields a multi-thousand-dollar annual salary lift that compounds for the rest of your career.

The thing everyone's worried about

AI & the network engineer

Two questions, both legitimate:

  1. Is AI going to replace network engineers?
  2. Is the CCNA worth studying for if (1) is true?

What the data says

Gartner (October 2024): Generative AI will require 80% of the engineering workforce to upskill through 2027.[15]

Gartner (November 2025): By 2030, the projection is that 0% of IT work will be done by humans without AI; 75% will be humans augmented by AI; 25% will be AI-only. Of 1.4 million layoffs tracked in 2025, fewer than 1% were attributed to AI productivity gains. AI is changing jobs faster than it's cutting them.[16]

McKinsey (2025): 32% of organizations predict 3%+ workforce reduction. The plurality predicts little or no effect. Software and data engineers are still the most in-demand roles.[17]

What AI is good at (in networking)

  • Generating boilerplate router/switch configs
  • Writing documentation
  • Suggesting fixes from log patterns
  • Correlating alerts at scale
  • Translating between syntax flavors (IOS → NX-OS → Junos)

What AI is bad at

  • Inferring intent. If 50 devices join a network at once, AI can tell you that. It can't tell you whether someone deployed a new pod or someone fat-fingered a config push.[18]
  • Navigating priorities. Budget, leadership, deadlines, vendor politics — AI doesn't get a seat at that table.[18]
  • Physical layer. AI cannot pull cable, replace a failed PSU, swap a transceiver, or trace a fiber path.
  • Novel topologies. Anything outside training data — bleeding-edge data center designs, custom fabrics, weird legacy interop.
"You're being elevated into a more strategic, more human, more impactful version of the job." — paraphrased consensus across Cisco's own blog, TechTarget, and Empowering Future Network Engineers.[19]

So is the CCNA still worth it? Yes, with a condition. CCNA + Python (the de facto language of automation) is one of the more AI-resistant skill stacks in IT. Pure click-ops work shrinks. Intent + design + automation + physical work stays human, possibly grows. The CCNA is the foundation that lets you have any of those conversations.[20]

Beyond the basics

Hidden hacks the forums actually use

Tricks people post on r/ccna and TechExams that aren't in any official study guide. Beyond Jeremy + Boson + Anki + labs.

  1. 30-minute timed bursts on a single skill — VLAN config in Packet Tracer, OSPF area drills, ACL builds. One Reddit user reported 40% faster OSPF resolution after switching to timed drills. Forces single-track focus and prevents mental fog.[23]
  2. Teach-back to a voice recorder on your commute. Explain OSPF, STP, or NAT out loud. Reported 28% retention boost. Works because explaining forces you to compress.[23]
  3. Hand-write configs before typing them. The CLI muscle memory locks in faster when you're slowed down by a pen.
  4. Daily 5-minute subnetting drill. Even when you "know it." The magic-number reflex degrades faster than topic knowledge. Five minutes a day keeps the speed.
  5. Build the Seven Second Subnetting table from memory at the start of every practice exam. By exam day, it's automatic — and gives you an instant lookup for the rest of the test.
  6. Mine forum pass-experience threads. Recent r/ccna passers describe themes (not exact questions — Cisco NDA), revealing which domains Cisco is currently weighting. Saves you from over-studying the wrong topic.
  7. Switch sources mid-study. If Jeremy's IT Lab puts you to sleep on STP, watch David Bombal or Keith Barker on the same topic. Different teaching cadences unlock different brains.
  8. Take the Cisco Modeling Labs free tier seriously. Most candidates default to Packet Tracer. CML is closer to real production gear and is free for the small-topology tier.
