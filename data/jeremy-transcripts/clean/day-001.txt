Network Devices — Day 1 study notes
====================================

Author-original summary covering the role and function of the five core
network components a CCNA candidate is expected to recognize on day one:
client, server, switch, router, and firewall. NOT a transcript of any
copyrighted source — this is a hand-written, paraphrased reference scoped
to objective 1.1 of the CCNA 200-301 blueprint.

[client-vs-server]
A network is two or more devices connected together so they can share
resources. The simplest pair is a client and a server. The client is the
device that asks for something — a web page, a file, a video stream. The
server is the device that has the thing and hands it back. Whether a box
is acting as a client or as a server is decided by the role it plays in
that single conversation, not by the hardware. Your laptop is a client
when you load YouTube. The same laptop becomes a server the moment a
friend on the same Wi-Fi pulls a file off it. The label is about the
direction of the request, not the make and model.

[switch-lan]
A switch connects many devices that all live on the same local network.
You plug PCs, printers, IP phones, and servers into it. Every switch
port is a separate connection, and inside one network the switch moves
frames from one port to another so that any device can reach any other
device. Switches typically have lots of ports — 24 and 48 are common —
and they are the device you use when the goal is "connect things that
sit next to each other." A switch knows how to reach every device in its
own LAN, but it does not, by itself, know how to reach the wider
internet. That is somebody else's job.

[router-internet]
A router is the device that connects different networks together. The
home network behind a router is one network. The internet on the other
side of the router is another. The router sits on the boundary, looks at
each packet, and decides which network the packet should be forwarded
into next. Compared to a switch, a router has fewer ports — often just
two or three — because it is not trying to connect a room full of
devices, it is trying to connect a network to one or more other
networks. If a switch's job is "find the right port on this LAN," the
router's job is "find the right network among many." Without a router,
your LAN cannot reach anything outside itself.

[firewall-security]
A firewall is the device that decides which traffic is allowed to cross
a boundary and which traffic is blocked. It looks at the source, the
destination, and the type of traffic, and it applies a set of rules:
permit this, deny that. There are two flavors a CCNA candidate should
recognize. A network firewall is a dedicated piece of hardware that
guards an entire network, usually placed next to or in front of a
router. A host firewall is software that runs on a single computer and
guards just that one machine. The job is the same in both cases —
separate "traffic we trust" from "traffic we do not" — but the scope is
different. A firewall is not how you connect devices. It is how you
control which devices are allowed to talk in the first place.

[common-traps]
The exam-style mistakes to avoid:
- A router does not exist to connect many PCs to each other. That is a
  switch. Routers connect networks to other networks.
- A firewall does not connect anything. It filters traffic. If a
  question asks which device "joins" or "links" devices, the firewall
  is wrong.
- A server is the role a device plays, not a category of network gear.
  Switches, routers, and firewalls are network devices. Servers are
  endpoints — the same kind of thing as clients.
- A switch alone cannot get you to the internet. You need a router for
  that. A switch that ports lights up does not equal "online."

[quick-recap]
- Client = the side that requests.
- Server = the side that responds.
- Switch = connects many devices inside one local network.
- Router = connects different networks to each other.
- Firewall = enforces which traffic is allowed and which is denied.

One-line master summary:
Client requests, server responds, switch connects locally, router
connects networks, firewall protects.
