Connecting apps to social media accounts is&nbsp;&nbsp;
another example. Many apps can be connected to&nbsp;
Instagram, Facebook, LinkedIn, etc. OAuth 2.0&nbsp;&nbsp;
lets these apps access your social media account&nbsp;

A third-party tool, like a scheduling app, can&nbsp;

In this example, this is Google’s OAuth service.&nbsp;&nbsp;
Finally, there is the resource server, the&nbsp;
server that hosts the protected resource. In&nbsp;&nbsp;
this example, it’s the server hosting the Google&nbsp;
calendar data that the client app wants to access.
Now let’s see OAuth2.0 in action. The overall&nbsp;
process consists of six main steps. First,&nbsp;&nbsp;
the client app requests authorization&nbsp;
from the resource owner. In our example,&nbsp;&nbsp;
this could be a scheduling app requesting&nbsp;
access to your Google Calendar. Next,&nbsp;&nbsp;
the resource owner—which is you, the account&nbsp;

Once permission is granted, the client app&nbsp;

your Google Calendar data. Finally, the resource&nbsp;
server validates the access token and provides the&nbsp;&nbsp;

the token is valid and the app has the correct&nbsp;&nbsp;
permissions, it will receive the data it needs.&nbsp;
The key part of OAuth 2.0 is the access token&nbsp;&nbsp;
issued in step 4. It functions like the tokens we&nbsp;
saw in bearer authentication. It grants access to&nbsp;&nbsp;
the specified resource within the allowed scope,&nbsp;
for example read-only access. And just as in&nbsp;&nbsp;

period of time without constantly requiring the&nbsp;
user to log in. So, that’s OAuth 2.0. It allows&nbsp;&nbsp;
third-party apps to securely access a user’s&nbsp;
data without exposing their credentials, and&nbsp;&nbsp;
while allowing users to control what information&nbsp;
