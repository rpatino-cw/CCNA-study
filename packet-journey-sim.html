How does a packet travel from your browser to a server?

Your app data gets wrapped with headers at every layer (7→1), pushed as bits onto the wire, routed hop-by-hop through IP/Ethernet devices, then unwrapped in reverse on the receiver. Reliable protocols like TCP add handshake, sequencing, and retransmit. UDP skips all of that and trades reliability for speed. TLS sits on top of TCP to encrypt the payload before it leaves your NIC.

Math

Core formulas

Three formulas underpin everything the simulator displays.

Throughput

T = Data ÷ Time

Effective bits delivered per second. Always ≤ link bandwidth; in the real world, loss and congestion pull it well below the ceiling.

Round-trip time

RTT = 2 × (dprop + dtrans + dproc + dqueue)

Four delay types, doubled because the packet goes and comes back. Queue delay dominates under load; propagation dominates over long distance.

Shannon capacity

C = B × log₂(1 + S/N)

Absolute maximum bits/sec a channel with bandwidth B and signal-to-noise ratio S/N can carry. Wireless dies here first.

Exploration activities

Structured experiments — each takes 3–5 minutes.

Activity 1 · TCP vs UDP

How much does reliability cost?

Run HTTP and then UDP back to back with loss at 10 %.

  1. What does each protocol do when a packet drops?
  2. Which one ever recovers? How?
  3. When would losing packets silently (UDP) actually be the right call?
Activity 2 · The TLS tax

Measure encryption's setup cost

Play HTTP and HTTPS under the same latency. Compare Sent and RTT.

  1. How many extra round trips does TLS cost before real data flows?
  2. Why did TLS 1.3 cut a round trip vs 1.2?
  3. Is the cost worth it on a 2 ms LAN? On a 200 ms satellite link?
Activity 3 · Latency scaling

Push the slider to 500 ms

Run each protocol at 10 ms, 100 ms, and 500 ms.

  1. Which protocol's RTT grows slowest? Fastest?
  2. At what latency does HTTPS start feeling broken?
  3. What would you tune on a high-latency link to mitigate it?
Activity 4 · DNS deep dive

Why UDP for lookups?

Run DNS repeatedly; watch the log.

  1. Why is DNS stateless and connectionless by default?
  2. When does DNS switch to TCP?
  3. What changes when the resolver caches the answer?

Watch out

Common mistakes

✗"TCP is always better than UDP."

Reality: TCP's handshake and ACK overhead destroy real-time apps. VoIP, gaming, DNS, and video conferencing rely on UDP precisely because late packets are worse than lost ones.

✗"HTTPS just encrypts HTTP."

Reality: HTTPS is HTTP wrapped in TLS, which also authenticates the server (via certificate), negotiates cipher suites, and derives session keys. Loss of TLS = loss of identity, not just privacy.

✗"Higher bandwidth means lower latency."

Reality: Bandwidth is capacity per second. Latency is delay per packet. A 10 Gbps transatlantic link still has ~70 ms propagation delay — physics doesn't care about your NIC.

✗"Firewalls operate at one layer."

Reality: Stateful firewalls track L3 (IP) and L4 (port + TCB), and NGFWs inspect L7 payloads. "Layer X firewall" is a simplification — most modern boxes span several.

✗"Packets always take the same path."

Reality: IP is best-effort. ECMP, BGP changes, link failures, and load-balancers can scatter packets of the same flow across different paths, arriving out of order.

✗"UDP has no header."

Reality: UDP has an 8-byte header — source port, destination port, length, and checksum. It's just minimal compared to TCP's 20+ bytes.

In practice

Real-world applications

🌐

CDNs

Cache content close to the user so RTT drops from "cross-continent" to "same city." Cloudflare, Akamai, Fastly.

🎮

Online gaming

UDP + custom reliability. ≤ 50 ms feels instant; ≥ 100 ms ruins competitive shooters.

📹

Video streaming

Adaptive bitrate over HTTPS. Short segments + HLS/DASH so one slow chunk doesn't stall playback.

💹

Financial trading

Microwave paths and FPGA NICs to shave microseconds. Co-location is literally "be closer to the exchange."

📡

IoT & sensors

CoAP over UDP, MQTT over TCP. Tradeoff: battery life (UDP) vs ordered delivery (TCP).

🔒

VPNs

Tunnel encapsulation — your packet becomes payload inside another packet. IPsec, WireGuard, OpenVPN.

⚖️

Load balancers

L4 LBs split by port; L7 LBs split by URL/host. Both rewrite headers that you'd see in this sim.
