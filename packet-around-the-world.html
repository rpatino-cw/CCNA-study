Packet Around the World

One packet. Nine hops. From Romeo's laptop in Algonquin, Illinois to a server in Tokyo. Read at your own pace. Replay until it sticks.

START

Romeo opens his laptop.

📍 Algonquin, Illinois  →  destination: tokyo-server.example.jp

Your laptop wants to load a webpage hosted in Tokyo. The server is roughly 6,300 miles away. The packet you're about to send will cross at least 6 different networks owned by different companies in 4 countries — and arrive in about 200 milliseconds.

Nobody coordinates this. There is no central traffic controller. Each hop just does its small job and hands the packet to the next one. That's the magic.

EncapsulationWrapping data in headers as it goes down the OSI layers. App data → TCP segment → IP packet → Ethernet frame → bits on the wire. happens before the packet leaves your laptop. The OS adds a TCP header (port 443 = HTTPS), an IP header (destination = Tokyo server's IP), and an Ethernet header — but only after asking one question first…

Slow-learner anchor Picture a letter in an envelope. Your laptop writes the letter (app data), seals it in a TCP envelope (port 443), puts that in an IP envelope (Tokyo address), then asks "where do I drop this in the mail?" — that question is the next hop.
HOP 01

Laptop → Home Router

💻 Romeo's laptop  →  🏠 Home router (TP-Link in the basement)

The laptop's IP is 192.168.1.42. The Tokyo server is on a totally different network. Your laptop's routing table says "if you don't know where to send it, send it to the default gatewayThe router IP a host uses for any destination outside its own subnet. Configured manually or via DHCP. Usually .1 of the local subnet." — which is your home router at 192.168.1.1.

But the laptop doesn't know the router's MAC address48-bit Layer-2 hardware address burned into every NIC. Used for forwarding inside one LAN. Format: aa:bb:cc:dd:ee:ff.. So it shouts on the LAN: "Whoever has 192.168.1.1, tell me your MAC!" That shout is ARPAddress Resolution Protocol. Maps an IP address to a MAC address inside one LAN. Broadcast question, unicast answer. Cached for ~4 hours by default.. The router replies with its MAC. The laptop caches it and sends the frame.

What device?
Home router (a multi-function box: router + switch + AP + DHCP server)
What layer?
Layer 2 (Ethernet frame to switch) + Layer 3 (IP packet to router)
What's it doing?
Receiving the frame, stripping Ethernet, looking up the destination IP, deciding the next hop
CCNA hook Inside one LAN = MAC addresses + ARP. Crossing to another LAN = need a router. The default gateway is the door out. Memorize that distinction — it's tested directly.
HOP 02

Home Router → Cable Modem → Comcast Edge

🏠 Home router  →  📡 Cable modem  →  🏢 Comcast neighborhood edge

Your home router does one critical thing before the packet leaves the house: NATNetwork Address Translation. Rewrites the source IP from your private 192.168.x.x to the public IP your ISP gave you. Without NAT, the Tokyo server couldn't reply — it can't route to a private IP.. It rewrites the source IP from 192.168.1.42 (private) to your public IP from Comcast (e.g. 73.45.12.88). That single trick is why home networks work at all.

The packet now travels over coax cable to the cable modemTranslates between Ethernet (your home) and DOCSIS (Comcast's coax network). Layer 1/2 device. The modern modem at the demarc point., then to a CMTSCable Modem Termination System. The aggregation box at the cable company that all neighborhood modems connect to. Where the ISP's network "starts." at Comcast's neighborhood node. From there, fiber.

The boundary between "your stuff" and "Comcast's stuff" is the demarcation point"Demarc." The legal/physical boundary between customer-owned equipment and ISP-owned equipment. On cable: usually the modem. On fiber: the ONT. On business circuits: a NID box. — usually the modem itself.

What device?
Cable modem at the demarc + CMTS at the neighborhood node + PE router at Comcast edge
What layer?
L1/L2 at the modem · L3 at the PE router (decides the next provider)
What's it doing?
NAT translates your private IP to public · DOCSIS carries the bits to the ISP · PE router accepts the packet into the Comcast network
CCNA hook "Where does my private IP become a public IP?" — at NAT, on the home router (or sometimes at the ISP). The Tokyo server will only ever see your public IP, never the 192.168 one. This is also why you can't host a server from home without port forwarding.
HOP 03

Comcast Edge → Equinix Chicago (IXP)

🏢 Comcast Chicago metro  →  🏛️ Equinix CH1, downtown Chicago

Comcast doesn't own the Internet. To reach a server in Tokyo, Comcast hands the packet to another network. The cheapest place to do that is at an IXPInternet Exchange Point. A neutral building where many ISPs and content providers connect to one big switch and exchange traffic with each other — usually for free. Equinix Chicago, DE-CIX Frankfurt, AMS-IX Amsterdam are major IXPs. — a giant building full of network gear where dozens of providers meet on a shared switch.

Equinix Chicago (CH1, on Cermak Road) is one of the biggest in North America. Inside, Comcast's router has a fiber crossing to a Tier-1 backbone like Lumen or AT&T. They peerTwo networks agreeing to exchange traffic directly. Settlement-free peering = neither pays. Paid peering = one pays the other for transit. with each other and run BGPBorder Gateway Protocol. The routing protocol of the Internet. Every ISP runs BGP with its peers to advertise which IP ranges it can reach. CCNA covers BGP at concept level only. to advertise which IPs each one can reach.

Each network has an AS numberAutonomous System number. Globally unique ID for a network on the Internet. Comcast = AS7922, Google = AS15169, CoreWeave = AS396107. BGP routes between AS numbers.. Comcast is AS7922. Lumen is AS3356. The packet is about to cross between ASes.

What device?
High-end edge routers from each ISP, all plugged into a shared switch fabric in the IXP
What layer?
Layer 3 — BGP runs between routers, advertising IP prefixes
What's it doing?
Comcast hands the packet to Lumen at the IXP because Lumen advertised "I can reach Tokyo's IP range" via BGP
CCNA hook The Internet is networks (AS numbers) connected to other networks via BGP. IXPs are the cheap meeting point. CCNA only tests BGP at concept level: "it's the routing protocol between ASes." Don't memorize attributes.
HOP 04

Tier 1 Backbone — Chicago to New York

🏛️ Equinix Chicago  →  🌆 Equinix NY4, Secaucus NJ

The packet is now on Lumen's Tier 1 backboneA network that can reach the entire Internet without paying anyone — only via settlement-free peering. There are roughly 16 Tier-1 networks worldwide. Lumen, AT&T, NTT, Telia, Tata, Cogent, Verizon, etc.. This is high-capacity fiber — typically 100 Gbps or 400 Gbps per link — running between major cities.

Inside the backbone, routers don't do full IP lookups for every packet. They use label-switchingMPLS. Each packet entering the backbone gets a small label. Core routers swap labels at line rate without looking at the IP header. Way faster than IP routing. Customer never sees the labels. (MPLS) to forward at line rate. The CCNA-level summary: backbone routers don't think hard about each packet, they just swap labels and push them down a pre-built path.

Chicago to NYC is roughly 800 miles. Speed of light through fiber adds about 13 ms latency. That's the minimum. No technology beats physics.

What device?
PE (Provider Edge) routers at each end · P (Provider Core) routers in the middle
What layer?
Layer 3 / "Layer 2.5" — MPLS sits between L2 and L3
What's it doing?
PE pushes a label · P routers swap labels hop by hop · PE at the far end pops the label and goes back to plain IP
CCNA hook P routers in the core don't know who you are. They just swap labels. PE routers at the edge are the only ones with customer state. This is why MPLS scales — the "thinking" lives at the edges, not the middle.
HOP 05

Submarine Cable — New York to London

🌆 NY landing station  →  🌊 transatlantic fiber  →  🇬🇧 London landing station

The Internet is mostly underwater. 99% of intercontinental traffic rides on submarine fiber-optic cablesGlass fiber bundles laid on the ocean floor, typically the diameter of a garden hose. Powered by repeaters every ~50–80 km. Lifespan ~25 years. There are ~500 active submarine cables worldwide carrying virtually all transcontinental Internet traffic. on the ocean floor. Satellites carry less than 1%. The packet you sent is about to cross the Atlantic on one of these cables — possibly MAREA, AEC-1, or a similar transatlantic system.

From a CCNA perspective, this is just another fiber link. The router doesn't care that the link goes underwater. It's still Ethernet over fiber. The label-switching keeps going. The packet keeps moving.

Latency: NY → London is about 3,500 miles. Fiber adds ~28 ms minimum. Real-world ping: ~70–80 ms because the path isn't perfectly straight.

What device?
Cable landing station = a building where the undersea cable terminates and connects to terrestrial fiber. Inside: optical amplifiers + routers.
What layer?
Layer 1 (the physical fiber) carrying Layer 2 frames carrying our Layer 3 packet
What's it doing?
Carrying photons across the ocean floor. The packet doesn't change. Only the medium changes.
Slow-learner anchor Don't overthink submarine cables. From the packet's view it's the same as any fiber — just very long. CCNA won't quiz cable names. It might quiz "what carries most intercontinental traffic?" Answer: undersea fiber, not satellite.
HOP 06

London → DE-CIX Frankfurt (IXP)

🇬🇧 Lumen London PE  →  🇩🇪 DE-CIX Frankfurt — biggest IXP in the world

Lumen reaches London but doesn't own much fiber further east. To reach Tokyo, the packet hands off again — to NTT, KDDI, or another Asian-Pacific carrier. The handoff happens at DE-CIX Frankfurt, the largest Internet Exchange in the world (handles >15 Tbps at peak).

Same story as Chicago: BGP, peering, AS-to-AS handoff. Different building, same shape. The Internet is the same shape everywhere.

This repetition is the whole CCNA insight: once you understand one hop, you understand all of them. BGP between ASes at every IXP. PE → P → PE on every backbone. NAT at every customer edge. The pattern repeats.

What device?
PE routers from two different Tier-1 carriers, both in the same IXP building
What layer?
L3 — BGP again. Same protocol, different continent.
What's it doing?
Lumen advertised the destination via BGP and routes the packet onto NTT's backbone — same dance as Chicago, on the other side of the Atlantic
CCNA hook Pattern recognition wins the exam. If you see "ISP A hands traffic to ISP B," the answer almost always involves BGP and an IXP. Memorize the pattern, not the cities.
HOP 07

NTT Backbone — Frankfurt to Tokyo

🇩🇪 Frankfurt  →  🌊 Eurasia subsea + terrestrial fiber  →  🇯🇵 Tokyo

NTT is one of the largest Asian Tier-1 backbones (AS2914 — sometimes called the "global IP network"). Their backbone reaches across Russia / through the Middle East via subsea cables / and lands in Japan.

The packet rides label-switched fiber across roughly 5,800 miles. This is the longest leg of the journey. Latency adds another ~70 ms.

By the time the packet reaches Tokyo, your laptop's TCP connection is still patiently waiting. TCP doesn't care how the packets got there — it just verifies they all arrived in order, and asks for retransmission if any are missing. TCPTransmission Control Protocol. Reliable, connection-oriented Layer 4 protocol. Handles ordering, retransmission, flow control. The packet's IP-layer journey is invisible to TCP — it just sees "data sent, data acknowledged." handles retransmits. Your laptop never knows the underlying chaos.

What device?
NTT's backbone routers — same PE/P architecture as Lumen, different continent
What layer?
L3 + MPLS · TCP at L4 patiently buffering on your laptop
What's it doing?
Forwarding at line rate using MPLS labels. Pure muscle work — no thinking about destinations.
CCNA hook Notice TCP showing up here. TCP lives at Layer 4 and runs end-to-end (your laptop ↔ Tokyo server). The intermediate routers don't touch TCP. They only look at the IP header. This is why "TCP is end-to-end, IP is hop-by-hop" is a CCNA mantra.
HOP 08

NTT Tokyo → Tokyo Office ISP → Office Router

🏢 NTT Tokyo PE  →  🏬 Tokyo office's ISP  →  🖥️ Tokyo office router

Mirror image of hops 2 and 3, in reverse. The packet leaves the Tier-1 backbone, enters a regional Japanese ISP (perhaps via another IXP — JPIX Tokyo, or BBIX), then enters the destination office's circuit. The office likely has fiber to the building (FTTB) with an ONTOptical Network Terminal. The fiber-equivalent of a cable modem. Converts light pulses on the fiber to Ethernet frames the customer's router can use. Sits at the demarc point on PON deployments. at the demarc.

Inside the office, traffic crosses an enterprise firewall that inspects the packet (allow port 443? scan for malware?) and finally reaches the office's Layer-3 switch.

The packet has now traveled through about 6 different companies' networks. It started in your laptop ~200 ms ago. Almost there.

What device?
ONT (fiber demarc) + CE router + enterprise firewall, all customer-side
What layer?
L1/L2 at ONT · L3 at CE router · L3-L7 at firewall
What's it doing?
Mirror of hop 2: ISP hands packet to customer, demarc converts media, customer router accepts traffic, firewall inspects and permits
CCNA hook Hop 8 is a mirror of hop 2. Modem (or ONT) at the demarc · CE router · firewall. The architecture repeats on both ends of any Internet conversation. If you understand one side, you understand both.
HOP 09

Office Router → Server

🖥️ Tokyo office router  →  🗄️ tokyo-server.example.jp at 203.0.113.55

The office router does the exact same thing your home router did at hop 1 — but in reverse. Strip the WAN encapsulation. Look at the destination IP. Realize 203.0.113.55 is on the local server VLAN. ARP for the server's MAC address. Encapsulate in a fresh Ethernet frame. Send it out the right port.

The server's NIC receives the frame. The OS decapsulates: Ethernet → IP → TCP → application. Apache (or Nginx, or whatever) sees the HTTP request. The journey is over.

Total elapsed time from your keystroke to the server reading your request: roughly 200 ms. About one-fifth of a second. For a packet that crossed two oceans and six networks. The Internet is, in fact, a miracle.

What device?
Office L3 router + L2 switch + the server NIC itself
What layer?
All of them. Decapsulation walks the OSI stack from L1 up to L7.
What's it doing?
Last ARP. Last Ethernet frame. NIC receives. OS strips headers. Application gets the request. Done.
The full pattern, one line: ARP → MAC → IP → label-switch → BGP → label-switch → IP → MAC → ARP. Edges think. Cores forward. TCP holds it all together end-to-end. That's the Internet in nine hops.

Two oceans. Six networks. Nine hops. ~200 milliseconds. That's how the world connects — and that's the entire CCNA Domain 1 backbone story in one packet.
